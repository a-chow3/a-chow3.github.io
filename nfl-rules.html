Analyzing Offensive Efficiency Trends in the Modern NFL

A decade of play by play data used to empirically test a loud media narrative: that the NFL's offense friendly rule changes have softened the game and driven a scoring explosion. The data tells a more disciplined story.

Author
Adam Chow, sole author
Stack
R · R Markdown · Plotly · Shiny
Data
NFL Savant · 2013 to 2023 (public)
Output
Knit HTML report and two Shiny apps
Charts below are rebuilt for the web in the same Okabe Ito palette as the report to walk through the analysis. The full self contained HTML report and two live Shiny apps are in the public repository. All data is public NFL Savant play by play from 2013 to 2023.

1. Project context

Over the past decade, NFL Commissioner Roger Goodell and the league's executive board have aggressively introduced rule changes with a dual motivation: improving player safety and increasing the entertainment value of the television product. Gallup documented a steady decline in football's popularity through the 2010s, which gave the league a reason to act. The vast majority of these changes have favored the offense, from defenseless player protections for receivers to the leaper ban on field goal blocks to moving the touchback to the 25 yard line. In March 2024 the league added new kickoff rules and eliminated the hip drop tackle, and the latter drew enormous backlash from fans, broadcasters, and former players. This project was built as a direct response to that discourse. Rather than accept the softening narrative at face value, it uses a decade of play by play data to test whether these changes really had the outsized effect on offense that has been so widely claimed.

2. Research questions

The analysis is organized around two questions. First, are the new rules measurably hurting the game, or is the offense explosion narrative overstated? Second, will the elimination of the hip drop tackle hurt defensive playmaking as much as critics suggest, given how defenses have historically adjusted to past rule changes?

3. Data source

The primary dataset is NFL Savant play by play data for regular season games from 2013 to 2023, covering every team and over a decade of game level detail. I deliberately chose play level data over season aggregates. Aggregates hide the within season and play type variation that these questions depend on, whereas play level data lets me subset flexibly by play type, direction, yard line, and time, and derive metrics like scoring percentage per drive and turnover percentage directly from raw events rather than trusting precomputed columns.

4. Technical pipeline

Everything is built in R, with R Markdown producing the final knit HTML report and Shiny powering two interactive apps. The core libraries are tidyverse and dplyr for wrangling, plotly for interactive hover and animation, ggplot2 for the static base layers, and reshape2 and tidyr for the long and wide transformations that panel visuals need. The processing runs from ingestion of the raw CSV, through cleaning and play type classification, into feature engineering that derives points per game by month and season, a play outcome classification, scoring percentage per drive, turnover percentage per team season, and yards allowed per play at the team season level. Those features are reshaped for faceting and animation, visualized, and finally deployed as a knit report plus two Shiny apps.

5. Structure and findings

The report is six sections, each building on the last to tell a coherent story about how offense and defense have actually evolved. The visuals below recreate each one.

Section 1 · Contemporary trends in the modern NFL

Average points scored by month across each season, the one place monthly granularity is used.

Insight: the monthly lens reveals the cyclical rhythm of scoring, defenses dominate early, offenses find their stride mid season, and production drops in January as playoff games thin out. Critically, there is no clear upward trend across the decade, which is the first strike against the scoring explosion narrative.

Section 2 · Evolution of play type efficiency Shiny app 1

Insight: boxplots of yards gained by direction, faceted by season in the live app. Rushing efficiency by direction has stayed essentially flat over the decade. The notable change is on passing: the lower tail on deep pass distributions disappeared between 2017 and 2021, a direct signal that the interception returned for negative yardage became far rarer once defenseless receiver protections were enforced.

Section 3 · Touchdown scoring trends by play type

Touchdowns by yard line, animated across seasons in the live report.

Insight: did the vanished negative deep pass plays turn into touchdowns? No. The deep pass outcome simply became more binary, complete or incomplete, without a measurable spike in scores. The disappearing negative plays resolved into incompletions, not points.

Section 4 · The trend of penalties

Specific penalty types by season, the potential hidden driver of the more offense narrative.

Insight: defensive pass interference, the only spot foul in the rulebook, did tick up and peaked at 335 calls in 2020, and penalty yardage spiked in 2019 and 2021 due to the magnitude of spot fouls rather than volume. Even so, penalties are not a significant driver of offensive production. Their overall influence is flat to declining.

Section 5 · Offensive and defensive play breakdown by team Shiny app 2

Total plays per team per season
Play composition (proportional)
Insight: the most structural finding in the project. Total plays per team have been declining, especially from 2019 onward, following a peak between 2013 and 2017, a dip in 2019, a spike in 2020, then a sustained decline through 2022 and 2023. Meanwhile the proportion of defensive playmaking events to total plays has held stable, meaning defenses have not been disproportionately disadvantaged.

Section 6 · Advanced defensive metrics and special teams

Yards per play allowed vs turnover rate, size = scoring rate allowed
Special teams volume by season
Insight: the median reference lines split team seasons into four quadrants. During the 2018 to 2021 window when defenseless receiver rules were being aggressively enforced, more teams sat above the median scoring rate allowed, so defenses did briefly struggle. By 2022 and 2023 they had found a new equilibrium and spread evenly around the median. Special teams mirror the plays decline: fewer possessions mean fewer field goals, extra points, and punts, and for the first time in the decade both field goal and extra point attempts fell below their long standing thresholds in 2022 and 2023.

6. Critical design choices

A few decisions did a lot of work. Choosing play level over aggregate data preserved the ability to subset by play type, direction, yard line, and time organically. Every visual uses the Okabe Ito colorblind safe palette so that readers with color vision deficiencies can distinguish every encoding. Section 1 is the only visual at monthly granularity, on purpose, because the within season rhythm of scoring is invisible at the season level. In the first Shiny app I split rush and pass into separate tabs rather than faceting them side by side, because the boxplots are dense and tabs enforce a focused comparison, especially on small screens. The median reference lines in the defensive scatter are not decorative, they create the four quadrants that make the animation's narrative readable. And I chose Plotly over ggplot as the primary framework for two reasons: its hover surfaces extra fields like team labels and exact yardage without cluttering the view, and its animation engine produces smoother transitions than gganimate, which rendered fragmented frames for this dataset.

7. Key findings

Scoring has not increased meaningfully despite the rule changes, so the scoring explosion narrative is not supported by the data. Defenseless receiver protections changed how deep passes resolve, not how often they score, turning old negative plays into incompletions rather than touchdowns. Penalties are not a hidden driver, their influence is declining rather than increasing. The total number of plays per team is falling league wide, a shift toward a slower, more possession centric game that explains fewer punts, field goals, and kickoffs better than any single rule. Defenses did briefly struggle to adapt between 2018 and 2021, then stabilized. And because teams have historically adapted to analogous rule changes within one or two seasons, the elimination of the hip drop tackle is unlikely to produce the catastrophic defensive impact the media predicted.

8. Final output and audience

The project delivers three outputs: a fully self contained knit HTML report with all static and Plotly visuals embedded inline, and two deployed Shiny apps, one for play type efficiency by season and direction, and one for team level offensive and defensive composition. It is written for three audiences at once: sports analytics students, data journalists, and quantitative football analysts who want an evidence based counterpoint to the narrative; general fans and broadcasters who want to know what the data actually shows; and data visualization practitioners interested in Shiny design, Plotly animation in R, and the tradeoffs between ggplot and Plotly.
