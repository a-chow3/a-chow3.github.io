3D Hand Angle Biomechanics Tool

A computer vision pipeline that reads any frame of a pitching video and returns the three wrist angles that matter for pitch design, backed by an interactive 3D skeleton that coaches use to teach.

The situation

I walked into my role at Tread Athletics and was dropped straight into the biomechanics department. The task was to build a hand angle function that, for any given frame of a pitching video, returns three wrist angles: pronation and supination, flexion and extension, and ulnar and radial deviation, each with its direction. To get there I had to learn what computer vision was, figure out how to import, cut, and store the videos, load the right models, and retrain the existing ones on baseball specific hand positions so the mapping was accurate. All of that in about a week.

What I actually built, and where I went past the ask

The only thing required of me was the function itself, three numbers and a direction. While I was building it I kept making this 3D skeleton plot as a check for myself, because seeing the hand in space told me which angle I was really calculating and which algebra to use. Partway through I realized the plot was worth far more than a private sanity check.

I always think about how I am going to explain a finding and get people to trust my method, so turning the numbers into something a coach could look at felt like the natural next step. I promoted the throwaway check into a real deliverable: an interactive 3D map of the mapped hand.

The result

At the end of the internship my boss thanked me for going above and beyond by building a 3D Plotly skeleton map that the coaches could put in front of pitchers, to show them exactly what their hand looked like in a given frame. The visual made the math believable, and believable is what gets a mechanical change to actually stick.

How the angles are computed

MediaPipe returns 21 hand landmarks. I add a forearm reference point, then build a local coordinate frame from the palm using the index and pinky knuckles and the wrist. Each wrist angle is the signed angle between the forearm and hand vectors projected onto the correct plane of that frame, so the numbers stay true to direction and are not thrown off by the size of the hand in the image.

The deliverable · dummy data

Interactive 3D hand skeleton

Pick a frame to see the mapped hand and its three wrist angles. Click and drag to rotate the skeleton, exactly like the tool the coaches used.

How to read it: the 21 joints and the forearm point form the skeleton. Each angle reads as a value plus a direction. For example a positive flexion means the wrist is bent forward toward the palm, while extension bends it back. Coaches compare the release frame against a target to guide a pitcher toward the hand position that gives a pitch its ideal shape.
