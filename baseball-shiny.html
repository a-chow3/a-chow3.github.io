UVA Baseball Performance Dashboard

The one stop shop that UVA Baseball uses to study hitting, pitching, and catching. Players and coaches move through matchups, zones, pitch arsenals, tendencies, locations, trends, and catcher framing, all in one interactive place.

Role
Student Director of Analytics, lead developer
Stack
R Shiny · TrackMan · ggplot2 · plotly
Audience
Players, pitching staff, coaches
Status
In production · Aug 2024 to present
Interactive demo below. The live dashboard runs on UVA's proprietary Master_CSV.csv tracking data. Everything on this page uses fabricated dummy players and numbers. The layout, the metrics, and every visual mirror the real tool, but no confidential data is present.

Purpose

UVA charts thousands of pitches and swings a season. On its own that data sits in a spreadsheet nobody opens. I built this dashboard so a hitter can walk in, pick their name, and immediately see how they do against lefties, where they punish the ball in the zone, and where they get beat. Pitchers get the same clarity on their arsenal, their tendencies by count, their command, and how their stuff is trending. It replaced long film and number sessions with a tool anyone on the team can read in a minute.

Target audience

Players first, coaches second. That shaped every design choice. The language is plain, the visuals are big, and the most useful view is always the default. A player should never need me in the room to understand their own page.

Methods

Daily TrackMan exports land in a cleaned master table. From there I compute the standard slash line and the modern batted ball and plate discipline metrics, split everything by handedness and by zone, profile each pitch by velocity, spin, movement, usage, command, and whiff, and score catcher framing as stolen and lost strikes translated into wOBA. The production tool also carries Umpire and Biomechanics modules, and the three shown here, Hitting, Pitching, and Catching, are the interactive core.

Results

The dashboard cut our analysis meeting time by more than half and gave the team a shared language for performance. During my time with the group the pitching staff climbed from seventh in the conference in ERA to second.

Explore the dashboard

Pick a module, then a player. Every table and chart recomputes for your selection, exactly like the production app.

How to read it: the full hitting board, sortable by any column. wOBA rolls all offensive value into one number where about .320 is average and .400 is elite. In Zone Swing, Chase, Whiff, and Pull Air describe swing decisions and contact quality that a slash line hides.

Batted Ball Spray Chart

Contact Map by Pitch Type

Individual Play Dataset

How to read it: the spray chart plots every batted ball by where it landed, colored by result, so pull and oppo tendencies jump out. The contact map shows those same balls back in the strike zone, colored by the pitch type that produced them, which is where you learn what a hitter can and cannot square up.
Swing decision rate by zone · catcher's view
Contact quality by zone · catcher's view
How to read it: both maps use the blue to red scale, where red is hotter for the hitter. The left map is swing, contact, or whiff rate by zone, the right map is exit velocity or quality of contact. Together they show what a hitter chases, what they punish, and where a pitcher should live.
How to read it: BF is batters faced. CSW is called strikes plus whiffs over pitches, a single stickiness number for a pitch mix. FPS is first pitch strike rate. Sort to find who misses bats, who fills the zone, and who does both.

Pitch Movement Profile

Pitch Usage vs LHH and RHH

How to read it: the movement plot shows how each pitch breaks relative to a spinless ball from the pitcher's view, with a dashed cloud for its spread. Pitches far apart give hitters different looks. The usage bars split how often each pitch is thrown to lefties versus righties.

Count State Transition Matrix

Net Run Value by Count

How to read it: the transition matrix shows the probability of the next pitch type given the last one, so predictable sequencing shows up as a hot cell. The count map lays every count in a diamond, sized and colored by the net run value the pitcher earns there, positive meaning runs saved.

Location Heat Maps by Pitch Type

Zone Distribution and Net Runs

How to read it: each small map is one pitch, shaded by where it lands most, with the strike zone outlined. The zone distribution buckets every pitch into Heart, Shadow, Chase, or Waste and shows the net runs earned in each, which is how you judge command and intent, not just raw location.

Pitch Performance Over Season

Single Game Decay

How to read it: the season view tracks a metric outing by outing for every pitch, with the bold markers as game averages. The decay view zooms into one start and one pitch, with a trend line, to catch the late game velocity dip that can flag fatigue before it becomes an injury.
How to read it: green stars are stolen strikes, borderline pitches the catcher framed into called strikes. Red crosses are strikes he lost. The panel converts those into wOBA saved, because a stolen strike changes the count and the count changes run expectancy. Net frames plus a positive wOBA per pitch is a catcher earning his spot on defense alone.
